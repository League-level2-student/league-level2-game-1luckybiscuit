GRAVITY LAD

by: Lukas Nepomuceno

This game was made for my level 2 project at The League of Amazing Programmers.
Click here to download and play!

1. Why I Made This Game

Way back when in the days of browser game websites like Armor Games, Nitrome and Miniclip, there existed a game (which still exists today) known as Gravity Guy. The mechanic, which is to switch gravity to avoid obstacles, was very compelling to my and I wanted to try a shot at it. So here we are.

2. What I Learned

User testing led me to make some quality-of-life changes and make the game fairer. I made the formations change faster so that each of them could be experienced without the pace becoming too boring, and I also found a problem that could end a run early regardless of the user's actions. Fixing it took ~2 months but it paid off since it allows the game to be challenging but fair, which was my goal.

3. Architecture of My Game

The objects in the game are the jumper, which the player controls, the floor blocks and the wall blocks. They are all block-shaped and block-like in class and code. The purpose of the walls and floors are for collision and obstacles. To emulate forward motion, all the blocks except for the player move left and off-screen. New blocks are generated on the right side outside of the screen. This creates a successful "snaking" effect.

4. Technical Implementation

The main mechanic in the game is the reversal of gravity. To achieve this effect, I used a binary variable called "gravity" and multiplied it to the velocity variable, which would reset whenever the player collided with a floor or ceiling. The velocity would count exponentially up, and would count in the other direction once the gravity changes from hitting the spacebar. This creates the rounded arc prevalent in real world gravity and makes a good challenge for the player to get used to and master it.

5. Most Challenging Part

The most challenging part of the project was fixing the walls and how they were generated. The biggest problem was that some vertical walls would be passable on a certain level of ceiling and floor. However, the ceiling and floor can change as close as 50 pixels after the wall, which can create a dead end and block the player. To fix this I tried many different, frustrating options with troubleshooting and debugging. In the end I found a simpler solution that created extra hitboxes for the walls based on whether they were on the top or the bottom. If a floor or ceiling was within this invisible hitbox, the wall would be shortened offscreen so that the player could progress without even knowing of the problem. It was unconventional and taught me a lot about errors in game development, but I'm glad I could finally fix it.

6. Future Fixes and Updates

I think the obstacles and formations are plentiful and very good being randomly generated, but some could use improvement. For example, the square bit obstacles are fairly easy to avoid on a flat surface, and the random "farlands" formations have many dead ends outside of the middle that may seem unfair. I would also change how the score was counted, probably giving an extra bonus for each formation conquered, like a level. As for aesthetic, I would make sprites for the blue blocks and make them look nice when overlapping. An interesting idea would be to also have to follow the game's vertical motion if blocks moved down or up while also moving left. Still, I am really happy with the game's current state.
